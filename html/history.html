Origins

The Vision of a God-King

Angkor Wat was built by King Suryavarman II, who reigned from approximately 1113 to 1150 AD. Unlike earlier Khmer kings who built temples to Shiva, Suryavarman II dedicated this monumental work to Vishnu — the Preserver — believing himself to be a divine incarnation of the deity.

Construction began around 1122 AD and required an estimated 300,000 workers and 6,000 elephants to transport the massive sandstone blocks from Phnom Kulen, some 50 kilometres away. The scale was unprecedented: the temple complex covers 400 acres, surrounded by a moat 190 metres wide and nearly 5 kilometres long.

The name "Angkor Wat" translates roughly as "City Temple" (Angkor from Sanskrit Nagara — City; Wat from Pāli Vatta — Temple). Yet its original Sanskrit name was Vrah Visnuloka — "the sacred dwelling of Vishnu" — reflecting its celestial aspirations from the very beginning.

Chronology

A Living Timeline

From divine commission to UNESCO recognition, Angkor Wat has witnessed the rise and fall of empires, religious transformation, and centuries of jungle reclamation before its return to the world stage.

c. 1113 AD
Construction Begins

King Suryavarman II commissions the temple as his state temple and eventual mausoleum, dedicated to Vishnu.

c. 1150 AD
Suryavarman II Dies

The king dies before full completion; construction continues under subsequent rulers. The temple becomes his mortuary shrine.

c. 1177 AD
Cham Invasion & Sack

The Cham people from what is now Vietnam sack Angkor, briefly occupying the Khmer capital and looting its treasures.

c. 1181 AD
Jayavarman VII Reconquers

The great king Jayavarman VII recaptures Angkor and begins a new era of even more ambitious construction, including Angkor Thom.

13th Century
Conversion to Buddhism

Angkor Wat transforms from a Hindu temple into a Theravada Buddhist shrine. Buddhist iconography is added throughout.

1431 AD
Khmer Empire Falls

Thai forces sack Angkor; the Khmer capital shifts southward to Phnom Penh. Angkor Wat remains inhabited by Buddhist monks.

1586 AD
Portuguese Discovery

Portuguese monk António da Madalena becomes one of the first Westerners to visit, describing it as "of such extraordinary construction that it is not possible to describe it with a pen."

1860 AD
French Rediscovery

Henri Mouhot, a French naturalist, brings Angkor Wat to wide Western attention, catalysing colonial-era restoration efforts.

1992 AD
UNESCO World Heritage

Angkor is inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, securing international funding and commitment for its preservation.

Key Fact

Angkor Wat is the only major Angkor monument to face west — the direction of the setting sun, associated with Vishnu and the realm of the dead, suggesting its dual role as a temple and mausoleum.

"One of these temples — a rival to that of Solomon, and erected by some ancient Michelangelo — might take an honourable place beside our most beautiful buildings."

— Henri Mouhot, French Naturalist, 1860

The Empire

The Khmer Civilisation

At its peak between the 9th and 15th centuries, the Khmer Empire encompassed much of mainland Southeast Asia — from the Malay Peninsula to southern China. It was one of the most sophisticated hydraulic civilisations in human history, engineering an elaborate network of reservoirs (barays) and canals to sustain a population of over one million in the Angkor region alone.

The city of Angkor itself, at its height around 1200 AD, was the largest pre-industrial urban centre in the world, covering an area comparable to modern-day Los Angeles.
