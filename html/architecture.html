The Design

A Temple-Mountain

Angkor Wat is the supreme expression of the Khmer temple-mountain concept — a physical representation of Mount Meru, the cosmic axis of Hindu-Buddhist cosmology, rising from a symbolic ocean toward the heavens.

Every element of Angkor Wat's layout carries profound cosmological meaning. The three-tiered pyramid represents the three realms of existence. The five towers mirror the five peaks of Meru. The surrounding moat symbolises the cosmic ocean. Even the cardinal orientation — facing west — marks its function as a site of worship and a tomb for the god-king.

01
The Quincunx

The Five Towers

The five iconic lotus-bud towers are Angkor Wat's most recognisable feature. Arranged in a quincunx — one at each corner and one crowning the centre — they represent the five peaks of Mount Meru. The central tower rises 65 metres (213 feet) above the ground, making it the tallest structure at Angkor.

Each tower's silhouette tapers upward in a series of diminishing tiers, decorated with fine-toothed decorative elements. The vertical emphasis is extraordinary — a visual aspiration toward the divine that is visible for miles across the surrounding plains.

02
The Bas-Reliefs

800 Metres of Story

The outer gallery walls are lined with the world's longest continuous bas-relief, stretching nearly 800 metres and standing 2 metres high. These extraordinary carvings narrate Hindu epics, Khmer military victories, and the cosmic drama of the universe.

The most celebrated panel depicts the "Churning of the Ocean of Milk" — a mythological event in which gods and demons cooperate to churn the cosmic sea, producing the elixir of immortality. Another epic panel stretches 49 metres and depicts the Battle of Kurukshetra from the Mahabharata.

03
The Apsaras

2,000 Celestial Dancers

Over 2,000 Apsara figures — celestial nymphs and divine dancers — are carved throughout the temple walls. No two are identical: each wears a unique hairstyle, jewellery, and posture, making them among the most detailed and sophisticated examples of Khmer sculptural art.

These ethereal figures were believed to inhabit the heavenly realm, serving as dancers and entertainers for the gods. Their graceful, sensuous forms represent the beauty of the divine world that Angkor Wat sought to replicate on earth.
